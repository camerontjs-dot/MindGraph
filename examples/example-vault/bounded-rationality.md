---
title: Bounded rationality
domain: decision-science
---

Herbert Simon coined this term to describe how people decide when full information and unlimited compute are not available. Rather than searching for an optimal choice, decision-makers tend to pick the first option that clears an acceptance threshold. Simon called the strategy satisficing, a portmanteau of satisfying and sufficing.

The behavior shows up across many domains. Where the textbook expects an optimizer, bounded rationality predicts a satisficer who stops as soon as good enough is found. Later work by Kahneman and Tversky on cognitive shortcuts built on the same observation from a different angle.

A satisficer is not irrational. They are economizing on a resource the optimizer pretends is free. The connection to [[feedback-loops]] (related) is that real decision-makers operate inside a balancing loop where search cost grows with each candidate evaluated.
